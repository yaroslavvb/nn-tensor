Worst-Case Tensor Approximation: L² vs Spectral

A report on what is provable about low-rank tensor approximation with no structural assumptions at all — the absolute worst case over every tensor. The answer depends violently on the norm: in Frobenius (L²) every format is hopeless and format choice is irrelevant; in the spectral norm a dimension-free rank ⌈1/ε²⌉ theorem holds for every tensor ever, and the CP-vs-TT-vs-Tucker ranking flips. Every rank, error, and greedy step below is computed live in your browser.

Part of the nn-tensor interactive tutorial (see also the CP-vs-TT depth-separation deep dive). Background results: Eckart–Young–Mirsky; de Silva & Lim 2008; Hillar & Lim 2013; Frieze & Kannan 1999 (the energy-decrement idea); Oseledets 2011 / Grasedyck 2010 (TT/HT formats).

Part 1 of 5

The setup: one question, two norms

Fix order \(d\), mode size \(n\), and normalize \(\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F = 1\). The question of this report:

The worst-case question. Given a parameter budget \(P\) and a format (CP, Tucker, TT), what error can be guaranteed for every tensor — \(\;\sup_{\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F=1}\; \min_{\mathcal S\,\in\,\text{format}(P)}\; \lVert \mathcal T - \mathcal S\rVert\;\) — and how does the answer depend on the norm \(\lVert\cdot\rVert\)?

Two norms matter. The Frobenius norm is the \(L^2\) norm of the entries — “total energy”: \(\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F^2 = \sum_{i_1\dots i_d} \mathcal T_{i_1\dots i_d}^2\). The spectral (injective) norm is the tensor analogue of the matrix operator norm — “the most any single rank-1 probe can see”:

$$\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_\sigma \;=\; \max_{\lVert u_k\rVert_2 = 1}\; \bigl\langle\, \mathcal T,\; u_1\otimes u_2\otimes\cdots\otimes u_d \,\bigr\rangle .$$

For matrices these are the Schatten-2 and Schatten-∞ norms and the SVD connects them. For tensors there are no singular values, but the two norms still bracket each other, \(\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_\sigma \le \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F \le n^{(d-1)/2}\,\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_\sigma\), and the spectral norm exactly measures one-step compressibility:

$$\min_{\text{rank-1 } \mathcal S}\;\lVert\mathcal T-\mathcal S\rVert_F^2 \;=\; \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F^2 - \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_\sigma^2 .$$

Part 2 of 5

L² (Frobenius) worst case: incompressibility, for every format equally

In Frobenius norm the absolute worst case has a complete and entirely negative answer, and it is format-agnostic. Any format with \(P\) parameters describes (the closure of) a \(P\)-dimensional set inside the \(N = n^d\)-dimensional tensor space. A covering / Gaussian-width argument then shows that a random unit tensor — which is essentially the worst case — has almost all of its energy orthogonal to any such set:

$$\text{energy captured}\;\lesssim\;\frac{P\,\log N}{N} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \text{worst-case } \lVert\mathcal T-\mathcal S\rVert_F \;\ge\; \sqrt{1 - \tilde O(P/N)}\,.$$

Nothing about CP vs TT vs Tucker enters: only the parameter count does. Concretely, the unfoldings of a random tensor have flat singular spectra (the matrix case of this is worked out quantitatively — quarter-circle law, exact error curve, asymptotics — in the Gaussian-noise deep dive), so the two-sided unfolding sandwich (see the main tutorial) forces TT bonds \(\sim n^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}\) for any constant error reduction, and the best rank-1 CP term captures only \(\Theta(dn)/n^{d}\) of the energy — versus \(1/n\) for the worst matrix. Exponentially worse, in every format.

Worst-case error vs parameter budget — the two norms side by side

Idealized worst-case guarantees (constants and log factors dropped): in Frobenius, error \(\approx\sqrt{\max(0,\,1-P/n^d)}\) — a cliff at \(P \approx n^d\), i.e. you get essentially nothing until you pay for every entry. In spectral norm (Part 3’s theorem), error \(\approx\min(1, \sqrt{dn/P})\) — falling from the very first parameters.

Bottom line for L². With no structure, low-rank tensor approximation does not exist as a phenomenon: worst-case (and typical) relative error stays near 1 until \(P \sim n^d\). Format choice is irrelevant. Compression theorems in Frobenius norm are always theorems about structure (smoothness, low exact rank, decay of unfolding spectra) — never about the worst case.

Part 3 of 5

Spectral norm: a dimension-free worst-case theorem

Measured in \(\lVert\cdot\rVert_\sigma\), the absolute worst case is not merely better — it is solved, by a three-line argument with no conditions on the tensor:

Theorem (greedy energy decrement; the Frieze–Kannan “weak regularity” mechanism). For every tensor \(\mathcal T\) of any order and any \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\), there exists \(\mathcal S\) with CP rank \(< 1/\varepsilon^2\) such that $$\lVert \mathcal T - \mathcal S\rVert_\sigma \;\le\; \varepsilon\,\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F .$$ Proof. While the residual \(\mathcal R\) has \(\lVert\mathcal R\rVert_\sigma > \varepsilon\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F\), take a unit rank-1 \(\mathcal U\) with \(\langle\mathcal R,\mathcal U\rangle = \lVert\mathcal R\rVert_\sigma\) and subtract the projection \(\langle\mathcal R,\mathcal U\rangle\,\mathcal U\). Each step removes \(\langle\mathcal R,\mathcal U\rangle^2 > \varepsilon^2\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F^2\) of Frobenius energy, and there is only \(\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F^2\) of it. ∎

The rank bound is independent of both \(n\) and \(d\). Running the same argument for \(t\) steps gives the envelope \(\min_{s\le t}\lVert\mathcal R_s\rVert_\sigma \le \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F/\sqrt{t}\), and the bound is tight: a matrix with \(K\) equal singular values (a “flat matrix”) keeps \(\lVert\mathcal R_t\rVert_\sigma = \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F/\sqrt{K}\) for exactly \(K\) steps — pinned at the envelope’s value at \(t=K\), so for every \(t\) the flat matrix with \(K = t\) matches the \(1/\sqrt t\) envelope and no uniform improvement is possible. Since matrices are order-2 tensors, no better worst-case rank than \(\Theta(1/\varepsilon^2)\) is possible at any order.

t = 0

Why is this consistent with Part 2’s hopelessness? Because they are the same fact. The tensors that are Frobenius-incompressible spread their energy so thin that every rank-1 probe sees almost nothing — but “every probe sees almost nothing” is the statement \(\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_\sigma \ll \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F\): such tensors are already spectral-norm-close to zero. A random Gaussian tensor has \(\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_\sigma/\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F \approx c\sqrt{dn}/n^{d/2}\) — within a \(\sqrt d\) factor of the minimum possible. One phenomenon, two norms, opposite verdicts.

Bonus: CP’s pathologies vanish here. The greedy decomposition always exists (no de Silva–Lim ill-posedness — we never ask for the best rank-R tensor), and it is well-conditioned: its coefficients satisfy \(\sum_t \lambda_t^2 \le \lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F^2\), so there are no diverging, cancelling components. The one thing that remains hard is the rank-1 step itself (NP-hard exactly; with an \(\alpha\)-approximate rank-1 oracle the same proof gives rank \(\le \alpha^2/\varepsilon^2\)).

Part 4 of 5

TT and Tucker in the spectral norm: upper bounds for free, lower bounds per cut

Upper bounds are inherited. The greedy output has CP rank \(R < 1/\varepsilon^2\), and a rank-\(R\) CPD embeds in every format: TT with all bonds \(\le R\), Tucker with all multilinear ranks \(\le \min(n, R)\). So TT and Tucker also achieve \(\varepsilon\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F\) spectral error, worst case, with constant (n-and-d-independent) ranks. The question is what they pay in parameters, and here the dense parameterizations differ sharply.

Lower bounds: every cut has its own adversary. Fix a target \(\varepsilon\) with \(K := \lceil 1/(2\varepsilon^2)\rceil \le n\), and pick any neighboring pair of modes \((k, k{+}1)\). Build the padded flat matrix: a rank-\(K\) matrix with \(K\) equal singular values placed across modes \(k,k{+}1\), tensored with unit vectors in every other mode. If \(\mathcal S\) approximates it within \(\varepsilon\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F\) in spectral norm, then contracting \(\mathcal S\) with the padding vectors (legitimate rank-1 probes!) leaves an \(n\times n\) matrix that must be \(\varepsilon\)-close in operator norm — and by Eckart–Young its rank, which is at most the TT bond \(r_k\) (and at most the mode-\(k\) Tucker rank), must be at least \(K\). Since this works for every \(k\):

Worst-case format costs in spectral norm (target \(\varepsilon\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F\); regime \(1/(2\varepsilon^2)\le n\); dense formats):
  • CP: rank \(\Theta(1/\varepsilon^2)\) → parameters \(O(dn/\varepsilon^{2})\).
  • TT: every bond \(\Theta(1/\varepsilon^2)\) → interior cores are \((1/\varepsilon^2)\times n\times(1/\varepsilon^2)\), parameters \(\Theta(dn/\varepsilon^{4})\).
  • Tucker: every multilinear rank \(\Theta(\min(n, 1/\varepsilon^2))\) → the core alone has \(\min(n,1/\varepsilon^{2})^{\,d}\) entries — exponential in \(d\).

The adversary, cut by cut

Slide the adversary along the chain: the flat matrix sits across one pair of modes and forces that bond to \(K\). The staircase shows the exact best spectral error of any approximant whose bond at the attacked cut is \(r\): it stays at \(1/\sqrt K > \varepsilon\) until \(r = K\), then drops to zero. A format must survive every adversary at once — so all its bonds pay.

The fine print. (1) The TT gap is partly an artifact of dense cores: converting a rank-\(R\) CPD to TT yields interior cores whose slices are diagonal — allowing that structure, “TT with CP-structured cores” matches CP’s \(O(dn/\varepsilon^2)\). The \(\varepsilon^{-4}\) is the price of the unstructured TT parameterization, not of the tree topology. (2) The lower bounds need \(1/(2\varepsilon^2)\le n\) (the adversary must fit in one mode); for smaller \(\varepsilon\) the forced ranks cap at \(n\) and the analysis transitions toward the Frobenius regime. (3) Everything here is existential: finding the greedy terms is NP-hard in general, and poly-time algorithms only approximate \(\lVert\cdot\rVert_\sigma\) within polynomial factors (roughly \(n^{1/4}\) for order 3 via sum-of-squares), with hardness evidence suggesting that is close to unavoidable.

The verdict: which format, when, and under which condition

Parameters needed vs target spectral error

Worst-case parameter cost to guarantee \(\lVert\mathcal T-\mathcal S\rVert_\sigma\le \varepsilon\lVert\mathcal T\rVert_F\) for every tensor, per format (dense parameterizations; each curve capped at the \(n^d\) cost of storing the tensor outright). Log–log: slope reads off the \(\varepsilon\)-exponent — 2 for CP, 4 for TT, \(2d\) for Tucker’s core.

Answers to the three questions

Under which condition can a worst-case approximation have few parameters in the spectral norm? No condition on the tensor — the guarantee is unconditional. The only conditions are on the target: the budget is \(P \approx dn/\varepsilon^2\), which is “small” precisely when \(\varepsilon \gg \sqrt{dn/n^{d}}\) — i.e. any accuracy coarser than the spectral-norm floor a random tensor already sits at. Push \(\varepsilon\) below that floor and you are asking for near-exact representation, where Part 2’s \(n^d\) wall returns. (And computationally: you need a rank-1 oracle; an \(\alpha\)-approximate one inflates the rank by \(\alpha^2\).)

Is CP, TT, or Tucker better in the worst case at a small parameter budget? In the spectral norm: CP, strictly — \(dn/\varepsilon^2\) versus \(dn/\varepsilon^4\) (dense TT) versus an \(\varepsilon^{-2d}\) core (Tucker). This is a complete reversal of the structured-Frobenius theory, where CP was the pathological format and the tree formats carried all the guarantees. The reason is geometric: worst-case spectral approximation is about additively accumulating a few good rank-1 probes, which is CP’s native shape. The multiplicative, entanglement-carrying structure that makes TT/HT exponentially powerful on structured tensors in L² buys nothing against a worst-case adversary in \(\lVert\cdot\rVert_\sigma\) — while its dense cores keep charging \(r^2\) per mode for it. In the Frobenius worst case: a tie (nobody compresses). If you care about small parameter counts and can only have a worst-case guarantee, you want the weak norm and the flat format; if you have structure and want \(L^2\) reconstruction, you want the tree formats — and the two recommendations are not in tension, because they answer different questions.

TL;DR. L² worst case: error \(\approx\sqrt{1-P/n^d}\) for every format — incompressible, format-irrelevant. Spectral worst case: rank \(\lceil 1/\varepsilon^2\rceil\) always suffices (tight, dimension-free); parameter ranking CP \(dn/\varepsilon^2\) ≺ TT \(dn/\varepsilon^4\) ≺ Tucker \(\varepsilon^{-2d}\), valid while \(1/(2\varepsilon^2)\le n\). Small parameters in the spectral norm are available unconditionally — for any tensor — as long as the accuracy target stays above the random-tensor floor \(\sim\sqrt{dn}/n^{d/2}\), and modulo the NP-hardness of the rank-1 step.
