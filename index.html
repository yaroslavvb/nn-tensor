Step 1 of 8

Tensors in sixty seconds

A tensor is just a multidimensional array. The number of dimensions is called its order (or the number of modes): a vector \(\boldsymbol{x}\) is an order-1 tensor, a matrix \(\boldsymbol{X}\) is order-2, and \(\mathcal{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{I\times J\times K}\) is an order-3 tensor whose entries are indexed as \(\mathcal{X}_{i,j,k}\). Slicing along modes gives fibers (fix all indices but one) and slices (fix all but two) — e.g. \(\mathcal{X}_{:,:,k}\) is the \(k\)-th frontal slice.

Why do these show up around neural networks? Everywhere: a convolution layer’s kernel is naturally an order-4 tensor (input channels × output channels × height × width), a batch of images is order-4, the joint action-value function of a multi-agent RL problem is a high-order tensor, and — as we’ll see — the derivatives and moments of a network are tensors whose structure reveals the network’s weights.

The curse of dimensionality. An order-\(d\) tensor with \(n\) entries per mode holds \(n^d\) numbers. At \(n=10,\ d=15\) that is already \(10^{15}\) — four petabytes as float32. Low-rank decompositions are the escape hatch: they represent big tensors with few parameters by exploiting structure, exactly like a low-rank factorization \(\boldsymbol{X}=\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{B}^\top\) does for matrices.

Step 2 of 8

The building block: rank-1 tensors

Every decomposition in this tutorial is assembled from one primitive: the outer product. For vectors \(\boldsymbol{a}\in\mathbb{R}^I\), \(\boldsymbol{b}\in\mathbb{R}^J\), \(\boldsymbol{c}\in\mathbb{R}^K\),

$$\mathcal{X} \;=\; \boldsymbol{a}\otimes\boldsymbol{b}\otimes\boldsymbol{c} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \mathcal{X}_{i,j,k} = a_i\, b_j\, c_k ,$$

an order-3 rank-1 tensor. It is maximally structured: every fiber in a given mode is a scaled copy of the same vector. (For matrices this is the familiar \(\boldsymbol{a}\otimes\boldsymbol{b}=\boldsymbol{a}\boldsymbol{b}^\top\).)

The tensor rank (CP rank) of a general tensor \(\mathcal{T}\) is the smallest number \(R\) of rank-1 tensors that sum to it. That single definition powers the first — and most important — decomposition of this tutorial.

Step 3 of 8

Canonical Polyadic Decomposition (CPD)

The CPD writes a tensor as a sum of \(R\) rank-1 components — the direct generalization of the low-rank matrix factorization \(\boldsymbol{X}=\sum_r \boldsymbol{a}_r\boldsymbol{b}_r^\top = \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{B}^\top\):

$$\mathcal{T} \;=\; \sum_{r=1}^{R} \boldsymbol{a}_r \otimes \boldsymbol{b}_r \otimes \boldsymbol{c}_r, \qquad \mathcal{T}_{ijk} = \sum_{r=1}^{R} A_{ir}B_{jr}C_{kr} \tag{paper eq. 1}$$

The vectors group into factor matrices \(\boldsymbol{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{I\times R}\), \(\boldsymbol{B}\in\mathbb{R}^{J\times R}\), \(\boldsymbol{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{K\times R}\) — one per mode. The minimal such \(R\) is the tensor rank. Storage drops from \(IJK\) to \(R(I{+}J{+}K)\) numbers.

Uniqueness — the CPD’s superpower

For order \(\ge 3\), the CPD is essentially unique under mild conditions: the only freedom is permuting the rank-1 components and rescaling within a component (e.g. \(\lambda\boldsymbol a_r,\ \tfrac1\lambda\boldsymbol b_r\)). Set \(R\ge 2\) above, then try both buttons: the factors visibly change, the tensor doesn’t — and nothing else preserves it. Matrices enjoy no such property (\(\boldsymbol{X}=\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{B}^\top =\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{Q}\,\boldsymbol{Q}^{-1}\boldsymbol{B}^\top\) for any invertible \(\boldsymbol{Q}\)). The earliest sufficient conditions are due to Kruskal — in the simplest form: \(\boldsymbol A,\boldsymbol B\) full column rank and \(\boldsymbol C\) without proportional columns. Uniqueness is what makes CPD factors interpretable — and it is exactly the property NN theory borrows to prove that network weights can be identified.

Rank can exceed dimensions

Unlike matrices (rank ≤ min(I, J)), tensor rank routinely exceeds every mode size — \(\boldsymbol{A}\) may have more columns than rows. A generic complex \(I\times I\times I\) tensor has rank \(\lceil I^3/(3I-2)\rceil\) — roughly \(I^2/3\) (over the reals several typical ranks can occur; this formula gives the smallest).

Computation: NP-hard, yet practical

Computing the CPD (and even deciding tensor rank) is NP-hard in general, and the best rank-R approximation can be ill-posed. In practice, block-coordinate schemes like the ALS you just ran work well, and under suitable conditions there are polynomial-time algebraic algorithms based on eigenvalue computations.

Generic uniqueness

Uniqueness is not fragile: a random rank-\(r\) cubic tensor is identifiable for almost all \(r\) below the generic rank — e.g. up to about \(\lceil I^3/(3I{-}2)\rceil - 1\) components for \(I\times I\times I\), far above what matrix intuition suggests.

Step 4 of 8

Tucker (multilinear) decomposition

The second classic format keeps a small core tensor and mixes it into each mode with a factor matrix:

$$\mathcal{T}_{ijk} \;=\; \sum_{r_1=1}^{R_1}\sum_{r_2=1}^{R_2}\sum_{r_3=1}^{R_3} \mathcal{G}_{r_1,r_2,r_3}\, U_{i,r_1}\, V_{j,r_2}\, W_{k,r_3} \tag{paper eq. 2}$$

with core \(\mathcal{G}\in\mathbb{R}^{R_1\times R_2\times R_3}\) and factors \(\boldsymbol U\in\mathbb{R}^{I\times R_1},\ \boldsymbol V\in\mathbb{R}^{J\times R_2},\ \boldsymbol W\in\mathbb{R}^{K\times R_3}\). The tuple \((R_1,R_2,R_3)\) — the multilinear ranks — equals the matrix ranks of the three matricizations (unfoldings) of \(\mathcal T\). Because \(R_n \le I_n\) always holds, a Tucker decomposition always exists — and the CPD is precisely the special case of a diagonal core: \(\mathcal G_{r_1 r_2 r_3} \ne 0\) only when \(r_1=r_2=r_3\).

Truncated HOSVD compressor

The tensor here is \(8\times8\times8\) with entries \(\mathcal T_{ijk} = 1/(1+x_i+y_j+z_k)\) — smooth, so its unfoldings have rapidly decaying spectra. Choose multilinear ranks and this page computes a real truncated HOSVD (higher-order SVD) in your browser: each factor comes from an SVD of one unfolding, so the whole thing is fast, stable linear algebra — no iterations, no luck.

What Tucker buys you. Computing Tucker approximations is a tractable, well-posed problem with efficient, stable SVD-based algorithms and well-understood approximation error — unlike the NP-hard CPD. That is why it is widely used to compress NN weights.
What it gives up. No uniqueness: insert any invertible \(\boldsymbol Q\) between the core and a factor (\(\boldsymbol U \!\to\! \boldsymbol U\boldsymbol Q\), core absorbs \(\boldsymbol Q^{-1}\)) and the tensor is unchanged. And the core itself has \(R_1R_2R_3\) entries — for an order-\(d\) tensor that becomes \(R^d\), exponential in \(d\). Fixing that blow-up is the job of the next section.

Step 5 of 8

Tensor networks: Tensor Train & Hierarchical Tucker

A tree tensor network generalizes Tucker by arranging factors as nodes of a tree and summing along every internal edge. Each edge carries a bond dimension (a rank); dangling edges are the tensor’s output indices. Two members of this family dominate practice: the Tensor Train (TT) — the minimal, linear tree — and the Hierarchical Tucker (HT) format, defined on a binary tree.

One family, three trees

Click through the formats. Hover a node to see what it is. Summation runs along solid internal edges; dangling edges are the output indices \(I_1,\dots,I_d\).

For the order-4 HT network above, the decomposition reads (paper eq. 3):

$$\mathcal{T}_{i_1,i_2,i_3,i_4} = \sum_{r_5,r_6}\mathcal G^{(3)}_{r_5,r_6} \Big(\sum_{r_1,r_2}\mathcal G^{(1)}_{r_5,r_1,r_2} U^{(1)}_{i_1,r_1}U^{(2)}_{i_2,r_2}\Big) \Big(\sum_{r_3,r_4}\mathcal G^{(2)}_{r_6,r_3,r_4} U^{(3)}_{i_3,r_3}U^{(4)}_{i_4,r_4}\Big).$$

Why trees beat one big core: storage

In TT and HT every node is a tensor of order at most 3, so storage grows linearly with the order \(d\) — while the full tensor and the Tucker core grow exponentially. This is the reason TT/HT are the formats of choice in high dimensions.

Storage explorer

The depth-efficiency connection. Coefficient tensors of deep sum-product networks correspond to (special cases of) HT/TT formats, while the CPD corresponds to a shallow one. A generic tensor representable by a deep (HT or TT) network of small size has CP rank — hence shallow-network size — exponentially larger. That is a theorem-grade version of “depth helps,” obtained purely from tensor rank comparisons. Like Tucker, tree formats are not unique, but they admit efficient and stable SVD-based algorithms, and their well-understood generic ranks translate directly into expressivity results for the corresponding networks.

Step 6 of 8

The extended family

Beyond the big three, the paper highlights several variants that carry the theory of NNs further. The most important is the symmetric CPD, because it is the format in which neural networks literally hand you their weights.

Symmetric CPD — and how it reads off a network’s weights

A symmetric CPD imposes the same factor in every mode: \(\mathcal T=\sum_r \lambda_r\, \boldsymbol a_r\otimes\boldsymbol a_r\otimes\boldsymbol a_r = \sum_r \lambda_r\,\boldsymbol a_r^{\otimes 3}\). It is stable under perturbations and admits polynomial-time algorithms for many rank regimes. Now take a 2-layer network \(f(\boldsymbol x)=\boldsymbol a_2^\top\sigma(\boldsymbol A_1\boldsymbol x + \boldsymbol b_1)\) and differentiate three times:

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol x}^{(3)} f(\boldsymbol x) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{d_1} \gamma^{(i)}(\boldsymbol x)\; \boldsymbol a_1^{(i)}\otimes\boldsymbol a_1^{(i)}\otimes\boldsymbol a_1^{(i)} \tag{paper eq. 9}$$

where \(\boldsymbol a_1^{(i)}\) is the \(i\)-th row of \(\boldsymbol A_1\) and \(\gamma^{(i)}(\boldsymbol x)\) collects \(a_{2,i}\,\sigma'''(\cdot)\). The third derivative is a symmetric CPD whose rank is the number of hidden neurons and whose factors are the first-layer weights. Because the CPD is unique, decomposing this tensor recovers \(\boldsymbol A_1\) — the seed of polynomial-time learning algorithms and identifiability results for 2-layer networks (with moments/score functions replacing derivatives when only data is available, via Stein’s lemma).

Block-term decomposition (BTD)

Replaces rank-1 components with low-rank blocks: \(\mathcal T=\sum_r \mathcal T_r\) with each \(\mathcal T_r\) itself low-rank. More flexible than CPD while keeping identifiability tools from algebraic geometry (most such additive formats fall under the umbrella of X-rank decompositions).

t-SVD (tubal rank)

A decomposition for multichannel/3-D data that is a special case of the BTD with a fixed factor. NNs with linear layers in this tubal format (“t-NN layers”) come with generalization bounds that improve as the tubal ranks shrink.

Paratuck-2

A non-additive generalization of the CPD with uniqueness guarantees. Its slices look like \(\ \boldsymbol A\,\mathrm{diag}(\cdot)\,\boldsymbol H\,\mathrm{diag}(\cdot)\, \boldsymbol B\). Exactly this pattern appears in the Jacobian of a 3-layer network (paper eq. 11): $$\nabla^{(1)} f(\boldsymbol x_n) = \boldsymbol A_3\, \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_2'(\boldsymbol z_n))\, \boldsymbol A_2\, \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1'(\boldsymbol t_n))\, \boldsymbol A_1 .$$ Hard to compute, but it extends derivative-based learning beyond two layers.

Tensor ring & friends

Closing the TT chain into a loop gives the tensor ring; it shows up when studying generative models with polynomial NN pushforwards, where method-of-moments learning turns out to be equivalent to a tensor-ring decomposition.

Step 7 of 8

Why neural networks care

The paper organizes the tensor–NN connection into four pillars:

1

Compression of weights

Reshape (“tensorize”) a weight matrix into a tensor, \(\mathrm{Ten}(\boldsymbol A_\ell)=\mathcal W_\ell\), then store it in CP/Tucker/TT format. Fewer parameters, faster inference — computations run in the decomposed format without ever reconstructing the full tensor. LoRA-style low-rank gradient updates are the matrix case; tensorized variants push compression further. Low-rank weights can even help generalization: bounds scale with the ranks, and training itself drifts toward low-rank solutions (“neural collapse”).

2

Expressivity & approximation

Sum-product networks (and polynomial NNs) compute \(f(\boldsymbol x)=\langle\mathcal A,\ \phi(x_1)\otimes\cdots\otimes\phi(x_{d_{\text{in}}})\rangle\) — an inner product between a rank-1 feature tensor and a coefficient tensor \(\mathcal A\). Choosing the format of \(\mathcal A\) chooses the architecture: CPD ↔ shallow, HT/TT ↔ deep. Rank comparisons between formats become width-vs-depth theorems, and TT-format approximation theory connects NNs to classical smoothness (Besov) spaces.

3

Learning with derivatives & moments

As you verified above: derivatives (or score-function moments, via Stein’s lemma \(\mathbb E\{f(\boldsymbol x)\otimes\mathcal S_m(\boldsymbol x)\} =\mathbb E\{\nabla^{(m)}f(\boldsymbol x)\}\)) expose NN weights as factors of a unique tensor decomposition. Consequences: polynomial-time learning of 2-layer (and some 3-layer) networks, sample-complexity guarantees, and parameter identifiability — with symmetric CPD the workhorse, Paratuck-2 for deeper models.

4

Emerging fronts

Generative models with polynomial-NN pushforwards (learned via moments ↔ tensor ring), hidden Markov models and RBMs as non-negative tensor networks, mixtures of linear classifiers identified through CP decompositions of conditional moments, and low-rank \(Q(\boldsymbol s,\boldsymbol u)\) tensors that cut sample complexity in reinforcement learning.

Why CP rank explodes while TT rank stays small

Worst-case approximation: L² vs spectral norm

What is provable about low-rank approximation with no structural assumptions. In Frobenius norm every format is equally hopeless and only the parameter count matters; in spectral norm a dimension-free rank-\(\lceil 1/\varepsilon^2\rceil\) theorem holds for every tensor ever — and the CP-vs-TT-vs-Tucker ranking flips. With a live greedy-approximation lab and per-cut adversary lower bounds.

Compressing pure noise

The exact best rank-\(r\) error of an \(n\times n\) Gaussian random matrix: Eckart–Young plus the quarter-circle law give a closed-form error curve with power-law asymptotics — spectral error \(\approx 0.785\,q\), Frobenius \(\approx 0.907\,q^{3/2}\) in the discarded fraction \(q\), and captured energy only \(4r/n\) at low rank. Live SVD simulations laid over the formula.

The 90° wall: approximation error as an angle

Measure error by the worst-case angle between \(Ax\) and \(Bx\) and low rank fails maximally: the sharp \(\arcsin\lVert(B-A)A^{-1}\rVert_2\) bound, an angular Eckart–Young theorem that degenerates to 90° at every deficient rank, and the escape routes — spectral gaps, relative conditioning \(\big(\sin\Theta = \frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\big)\), and typical-case inputs.
