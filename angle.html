The 90° Wall: Measuring Approximation Error by Angle

Norms ask how far \(Bx\) is from \(Ax\). Suppose you instead care whether \(Bx\) points the way \(Ax\) points — the worst-case angle \(\Theta(A,B) = \sup_x \angle(Ax, Bx)\) over all inputs. This metric has a name (it is the operator angle of the relative operator \(BA^{-1}\), the arccos of an antieigenvalue), it has exactly one general bound (an arcsine of the relative error), and it has a brutal no-go: every low-rank approximation of an invertible matrix is 90° wrong somewhere — the angular Eckart–Young theorem exists and is degenerate. Every curve below is computed live in your browser.

Part 1 of 5

The metric: worst-case output angle

For \(A, B \in \mathbb R^{m\times n}\), define the worst-case angular error

$$\Theta(A,B) \;=\; \sup_{\substack{x \ne 0 \\ Ax \ne 0,\; Bx \ne 0}} \angle(Ax, Bx), \qquad \angle(u,v) = \arccos\frac{u^{\mathsf T} v}{\lVert u\rVert\,\lVert v\rVert} \in [0, \pi].$$

If \(A\) is invertible, substitute \(y = Ax\): the two-matrix problem collapses to a one-operator problem,

$$\Theta(A,B) \;=\; \sup_{y \ne 0}\; \angle(y,\, Cy), \qquad C = BA^{-1},$$

— how far the relative operator \(C\) turns vectors. This quantity is the operator angle (also "singular angle", "maximal turning angle") of \(C\); its cosine, \(\inf_y \langle y, Cy\rangle / \lVert y\rVert\lVert Cy\rVert\), is the first antieigenvalue in Gustafson's operator trigonometry (for two matrices, an interaction antieigenvalue). The terminology is not fully standardized — the concept is.

Two things to internalize before bounding it. First, \(\Theta\) is not a norm and cannot be expressed through \(\lVert A - B\rVert\) alone: \(\Theta(A, 100A) = 0\) while \(\lVert A - 100A\rVert\) is huge — angle sees direction, not gain. Second, if the sign of the output is also irrelevant, use the projective variant \(\Theta_{\mathrm{proj}}\) with \(\lvert\cos\rvert\) in the definition; it measures output lines and satisfies \(\sin \Theta_{\mathrm{proj}} = \sup_x \lVert P_{Ax} - P_{Bx}\rVert_2\), the worst-case gap between the rank-one output projectors.

Angle vs input direction, and why no norm can see it

A fixed \(2\times2\) matrix \(A\), an approximation \(B = \alpha\,(A + \varepsilon E)\): the curve is \(\angle(Ax, Bx)\) as the input direction sweeps a half-circle. Drag \(\varepsilon\) to inject genuine directional error. Then drag the scale \(\alpha\): \(\lVert A - B\rVert_2\) changes wildly, the angle curve does not move a pixel.

What the metric sees. \(\Theta\) is blind to any positive rescaling of \(B\) (or of \(A\)) — it measures the direction field \(x \mapsto Bx/\lVert Bx\rVert\) against \(x \mapsto Ax/\lVert Ax\rVert\) and nothing else. That makes it the right error for "does the approximation send inputs the right way" questions (classifiers before the softmax, matched filters, preconditioned directions) and the wrong error whenever magnitudes matter.

Part 2 of 5

The one general bound: arcsin of the relative error

Write \(E = B - A\), so \(Bx = Ax + Ex\). If the perturbation is uniformly small relative to the output it perturbs,

$$\eta \;=\; \sup_{x\ne 0} \frac{\lVert Ex\rVert}{\lVert Ax\rVert} \;=\; \lVert (B-A)A^{-1}\rVert_2 \;\lt\; 1,$$

then \(Bx\) lives in a ball of radius \(\eta\lVert Ax\rVert\) around \(Ax\), and the worst direction in that ball is the tangent ray:

$$\boxed{\;\Theta(A,B) \;\le\; \arcsin \eta\;}$$

and the bound is sharp — equality holds the moment some \(Ex\) of maximal relative size is orthogonal to \(Ax\). Weakening \(\eta\) through \(\lVert EA^{-1}\rVert_2 \le \lVert E\rVert_2 / \sigma_{\min}(A)\) gives the conventional chain

$$\Theta(A,B) \;\le\; \arcsin\frac{\lVert A-B\rVert_2}{\sigma_{\min}(A)} \;=\; \arcsin\Big(\kappa_2(A)\cdot\frac{\lVert A-B\rVert_2}{\lVert A\rVert_2}\Big),$$

and since angles ignore scale, you may optimize the free scalar first: \(\Theta \le \arcsin\big(\inf_{\alpha>0}\lVert \alpha BA^{-1} - I\rVert_2\big)\) — that is the dashed line the Part 1 widget draws. The lesson sits in the \(\kappa\): angular accuracy is a relative, condition-number-sensitive requirement. The classic two-line disaster:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & \epsilon\end{pmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}:\qquad \lVert A - B\rVert_2 = \epsilon \to 0, \quad\text{yet}\quad \Theta(A,B) = 90^\circ \;\;\text{(the direction } e_2 \text{ is annihilated).}$$

The tangent-ball picture

Everything about the bound in one drawing: \(Bx\) is confined to the ball of radius \(\eta\lVert Ax\rVert\) at the tip of \(Ax\); the extreme direction is the tangent from the origin, at angle \(\arcsin\eta\). Drag \(\eta\) past \(1\) and the ball swallows the origin — the cone becomes the whole plane and no angular guarantee survives.

Why this is expensive. The bound needs the error small against \(\sigma_{\min}(A)\), the weakest output of \(A\) — precisely the part every norm-based approximation is happiest to throw away. For an \(n\times n\) Gaussian matrix \(\kappa_2 \sim n\) (Edelman), so a uniform \(5^\circ\) guarantee needs relative spectral error \(\lesssim 0.087/n\) — not "a few percent", but "a few percent of \(1/n\)". And no low-rank \(B\) can ever reach \(\eta \lt 1\): on \(\ker B\) the ratio \(\lVert Ex\rVert/\lVert Ax\rVert\) equals \(1\) exactly. That observation becomes a theorem next.

Part 3 of 5

Low rank hits the wall: Eckart–Young degenerates at 90°

Try the best candidate first. For the rank-\(r\) truncated SVD \(A_r\), the kept and discarded parts are orthogonal for every input: \(Ax = A_r x + A_{\perp}x\) with \(A_r x \perp A_\perp x\). The angle comes out in closed form — in singular coordinates \(c_j = v_j^{\mathsf T}x\),

$$\tan \angle(Ax, A_r x) = \frac{\lVert A_\perp x\rVert}{\lVert A_r x\rVert} = \Bigg(\frac{\sum_{j>r}\sigma_j^2 c_j^2}{\sum_{j\le r}\sigma_j^2 c_j^2}\Bigg)^{1/2}, \qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad \sin \angle(Ax, A_r x) = \frac{\lVert (A - A_r)x\rVert}{\lVert Ax\rVert},$$

so for the truncated SVD the angle at \(x\) is exactly the arcsine of the pointwise relative error — Part 2's bound holds with equality everywhere. Now feed it the adversarial input \(x_\varepsilon = v_{r+1} + \varepsilon v_1\): almost all of it lies in the discarded subspace, with an \(\varepsilon\)-whisker of signal to keep \(A_r x \neq 0\). Then

$$\angle(Ax_\varepsilon, A_r x_\varepsilon) = \arctan\frac{\sigma_{r+1}}{\varepsilon\,\sigma_1} \;\longrightarrow\; 90^\circ \quad (\varepsilon \to 0).$$

So \(\Theta(A, A_r) = 90^\circ\) for every \(r \lt \operatorname{rank} A\). And no cleverer \(B\) escapes: any \(B\) with \(\operatorname{rank} B \le r \lt n\) has a nontrivial kernel; walk \(x = x_0 + \delta z\) with \(x_0 \in \ker B\), and as \(\delta \to 0\) the output \(Bx = \delta Bz\) points anywhere in \(\operatorname{range} B\) while \(Ax \to Ax_0 \neq 0\) — flipping \(z \mapsto -z\) forces an angle \(\ge 90^\circ\). Hence the angular Eckart–Young theorem, in degenerate form:

$$\inf_{\operatorname{rank} B \le r} \Theta(A,B) \;=\; \frac{\pi}{2} \qquad\text{for every } 1 \le r \lt n,\; A \text{ invertible.}$$

The truncated SVD attains the optimum — and the optimum says maximally wrong somewhere. (Its one virtue: \(\langle Ax, A_r x\rangle = \lVert A_r x\rVert^2 \ge 0\), so it is never obtuse; a careless low-rank \(B\) can exceed \(90^\circ\).) The wall is a worst-case statement, though. For a random unit input, \(c_j^2 \approx 1/n\) concentrates and the tan-formula turns into the tail-to-head energy ratio:

$$\sin \angle(Ax, A_r x) \;\approx\; \mathcal E_F(r) \qquad\text{— the typical angle is the arcsine of the relative Frobenius error,}$$

i.e. the quarter-circle curve from the Gaussian page, read through an arcsine.

The wall vs the crowd, live on a Gaussian matrix

Singular values of a fresh \(100\times100\) Gaussian sample drive both curves. The adversarial input \(v_{r+1} + \varepsilon v_1\) gives \(\arctan(\sigma_{r+1}/\varepsilon\sigma_1)\) — drag \(\varepsilon\) toward zero and watch it flatten into the 90° wall at every rank. Random inputs (median of 400 samples) instead track \(\arcsin \mathcal E_F(r)\) (dashed): the Frobenius error curve in angular disguise.

How big is "somewhere"? The near-90° inputs occupy a thin sliver — vectors within \(\sim\varepsilon\) of the discarded subspace — and random sampling essentially never finds them (the widget's median curve is oblivious). But "rare under the uniform measure" is not "ignorable": optimizers, adversarial attacks, feedback loops, and iterative solvers all concentrate mass exactly where an approximation is weakest. Worst-case metrics exist because inputs are not always random.

Part 4 of 5

Escape routes: what actually controls angles

Route 1 — restrict the inputs. The wall is built from inputs that hide in the discarded subspace, so promise they can't: require every admissible unit input to keep mass \(\lVert P_r x\rVert \ge \gamma\) in the retained right-singular subspace. Then \(\lVert A_r x\rVert \ge \sigma_r \gamma\) and \(\lVert A_\perp x\rVert \le \sigma_{r+1}\sqrt{1-\gamma^2}\), so

$$\Theta_{\mathcal S}(A, A_r) \;\le\; \arctan\Big[\frac{\sigma_{r+1}}{\sigma_r}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{1-\gamma^2}}{\gamma}\Big].$$

The bound factors into what you promise (\(\gamma\)) and what the matrix offers (the gap ratio \(\sigma_{r+1}/\sigma_r\)). For noise the matrix offers nothing — the spectrum is gapless, \(\sigma_{r+1}/\sigma_r \approx 1\), and the bound collapses to \(\arccos\gamma\): you get out exactly the concentration you put in. A genuine spectral gap is the only thing that beats your promise:

The restricted-input bound: promise vs gap

The guaranteed angle \(\arctan[(\sigma_{r+1}/\sigma_r)\sqrt{1-\gamma^2}/\gamma]\) at every rank, on the live Gaussian sample from Part 3. Toggle in a planted rank-20 signal (top 20 singular values × 8) and watch the bound crater exactly at \(r=20\) — a spectral gap converts input concentration into angular accuracy at a discount.

Route 2 — keep full rank and condition the relative operator. Angles are multiplicative, so the right target is not \(B \approx A\) but \(C = BA^{-1} \approx\) (a positive multiple of) \(I\). In the cleanest case — \(C\) symmetric positive definite with eigenvalues in \([m, M]\) — the worst angle is known exactly (Kantorovich's inequality, Gustafson's first antieigenvalue):

$$\cos\Theta = \frac{2\sqrt{mM}}{m+M}, \qquad \boxed{\;\sin\Theta = \frac{M-m}{M+m} = \frac{\kappa_2(C) - 1}{\kappa_2(C) + 1}\;}$$

Note what is absent: any closeness of \(B\) to \(A\). \(B\) may be numerically far away — it must only distort \(A\)'s output directions by nearly the same relative factor in every direction. This is precisely the preconditioning philosophy: a good preconditioner \(M^{-1}\) makes \(\kappa(M^{-1}A) \approx 1\), i.e. makes the relative operator turn nothing.

Exact angle from relative conditioning

The full-rank trade: how well-conditioned must the relative operator \(BA^{-1}\) be (SPD case) for a given worst-case angle?

Route 3 — change the question. If your inputs genuinely are generic, Part 3 already gave the answer: the typical angle is \(\arcsin\mathcal E_F(r)\), a perfectly tame, rank-responsive quantity. Worst-case angle is the wrong metric for average-case pipelines — just say so explicitly, and use the Frobenius curve.

The three knobs. (1) Restrict inputs: concentration \(\gamma\) in the kept subspace buys \(\arctan[(\sigma_{r+1}/\sigma_r)\sqrt{1-\gamma^2}/\gamma]\) — your promise times the matrix's gap. (2) Keep full rank: conditioning of \(BA^{-1}\) buys \(\arcsin\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\), no closeness to \(A\) required. (3) Go typical-case: random inputs see \(\arcsin\mathcal E_F(r)\). What does not work: any amount of additive norm accuracy above \(\sigma_{\min}\), and any low-rank \(B\) on unrestricted inputs.

Part 5 of 5

The verdict: angle is a multiplicative metric in an additive world

Three ways to read this page. As geometry: norm approximation controls \(B - A\); angular approximation controls \(BA^{-1} - I\). The first is additive and blind to \(A\)'s weak directions; the second is multiplicative and weights every direction of \(A\) equally — including \(\sigma_{\min}\)-scale outputs that additive metrics happily sacrifice. As an impossibility theorem: rank deficiency is a kernel, a kernel is a direction whose output can point anywhere, so worst-case angle and low-rank approximation are simply incompatible — the angular Eckart–Young optimum exists and equals \(90^\circ\) at every deficient rank. As practical advice: compressing a layer or an operator by truncated SVD is safe for typical directions (\(\arcsin\mathcal E_F\), smoothly decaying in rank) and unsafe against any process that can seek out the discarded subspace; if directions must be preserved uniformly, the budget question is not "how many singular values" but "how well can I condition \(BA^{-1}\)".

TL;DR. Worst-case output angle \(\Theta(A,B) = \sup_x \angle(Ax,Bx)\) is the operator angle of \(BA^{-1}\) (arccos of an antieigenvalue), not a function of any norm of \(A-B\). The sharp general bound is \(\Theta \le \arcsin\lVert(B-A)A^{-1}\rVert_2 \le \arcsin(\kappa_2(A)\,\lVert A-B\rVert_2/\lVert A\rVert_2)\) — angular accuracy is relative accuracy. Low rank fails it maximally: \(\inf_{\operatorname{rank}\le r}\Theta = 90^\circ\) for every \(r \lt n\), attained by the truncated SVD via inputs \(v_{r+1} + \varepsilon v_1\). Typical inputs instead see \(\arcsin\mathcal E_F(r)\); restricted inputs see \(\arctan[(\sigma_{r+1}/\sigma_r)\sqrt{1-\gamma^2}/\gamma]\); and a full-rank SPD relative operator sees exactly \(\arcsin\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\). Rank buys norms; only conditioning buys angles.
