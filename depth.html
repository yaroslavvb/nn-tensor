Why CP Rank Explodes

A generic tensor with tiny Tensor-Train (or Hierarchical-Tucker) ranks has exponentially large CP rank. This page walks through the entire argument — one tool, one worked example, one theorem — with every rank computed live in your browser. It is the tensor-algebra fact behind “deep sum-product networks are exponentially more efficient than shallow ones.”

A companion deep dive to the interactive tensor-decompositions tutorial, based on arXiv:2508.18408 (Sec. II-D & V-B) and the depth-separation results of Cohen–Sharir–Shashua (COLT 2016) and Khrulkov–Novikov–Oseledets (ICLR 2018) cited there.

Part 1 of 8

The claim, precisely

Recall the two formats (details in the main tutorial). The CP rank of an order-\(d\) tensor is the smallest \(R\) with

$$\mathcal T \;=\; \sum_{r=1}^{R} \boldsymbol a_r^{(1)}\otimes \boldsymbol a_r^{(2)}\otimes\cdots\otimes \boldsymbol a_r^{(d)},$$

and the TT (tensor-train) ranks \((r_1,\dots,r_{d-1})\) are the smallest bond dimensions for which \(\mathcal T\) is a chain of small cores. Two facts, in opposite directions:

Cheap direction. Every TT rank is at most the CP rank: \(r_k \le \operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal T)\) for all \(k\). A deep format can always absorb a shallow one with no blow-up. (Proved in Part 3 — it is a one-liner once we have the tool.)
Exponential direction. For almost every tensor with mode sizes \(\ge r\) whose TT (or HT) ranks are bounded by \(r\), the CP rank is at least \(r^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}\) — exponential in the order \(d\). Small-TT does not imply small-CP; it fails as badly as it possibly could.

The whole argument fits in four moves, and each one is interactive below:

  1. One tool: matricization ranks lower-bound CP rank — for every way of splitting the modes into two groups (Part 2).
  2. What TT controls: TT ranks are exactly the ranks of the \(d-1\) contiguous splits — a vanishing fraction of all \(2^{d-1}-1\) splits (Part 3).
  3. A worked example: the “chain of links” tensor — \(d\) links, order \(2d\) — TT ranks \(\le 2\), yet one crossing split is a giant identity matrix, forcing CP rank \(2^{d} = 2^{\mathrm{order}/2}\) (Parts 4–5).
  4. The theorem: generically, the same crossing split is as large as it can be, giving the \(r^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}\) bound (Part 6). Then we translate it back to neural networks (Part 7).

Part 2 of 8

The one tool: bipartition unfoldings

Pick any subset \(S\subseteq\{1,\dots,d\}\) of the modes. Grouping the indices in \(S\) into a row index and the rest into a column index flattens \(\mathcal T\) into the unfolding matrix

$$\mathcal T_{\langle S\rangle}\;\in\; \mathbb R^{\left(\prod_{k\in S} n_k\right)\times\left(\prod_{k\notin S} n_k\right)} ,$$

with the same entries, just reshaped. Nothing is lost — only regrouped. Now watch what reshaping does to a rank-1 tensor:

$$\bigl(\boldsymbol a^{(1)}\otimes\cdots\otimes \boldsymbol a^{(d)}\bigr)_{\langle S\rangle} \;=\;\Bigl(\textstyle\bigotimes_{k\in S} \boldsymbol a^{(k)}\Bigr) \Bigl(\textstyle\bigotimes_{k\notin S} \boldsymbol a^{(k)}\Bigr)^{\!\top}.$$

The factors regroup into one long row-vector and one long column-vector — a rank-1 matrix, no matter which \(S\) you chose. Summing \(R\) such terms gives a matrix of rank at most \(R\). That is the entire proof of:

Lemma (the only tool we need). For every bipartition \(S\): \(\ \operatorname{rank}\bigl(\mathcal T_{\langle S\rangle}\bigr)\;\le\; \operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal T)\). In words: a low-CP-rank tensor cannot have any high-rank unfolding — under any regrouping whatsoever. To prove CP rank is huge, exhibit one huge unfolding.

Part 3 of 8

What TT controls: contiguous cuts only

A TT decomposition writes each entry as a product of small matrices — one core per mode (physicists call the same object a matrix-product state):

$$\mathcal T_{i_1,i_2,\dots,i_d} \;=\; \underbrace{\boldsymbol G_1[i_1]}_{1\times r_1}\, \underbrace{\boldsymbol G_2[i_2]}_{r_1\times r_2}\cdots \underbrace{\boldsymbol G_d[i_d]}_{r_{d-1}\times 1}.$$

The fundamental fact about this format (it is what the TT-SVD algorithm computes) is:

TT ranks = contiguous-cut ranks. The minimal bond dimension \(r_k\) equals the rank of the unfolding \(\mathcal T_{\langle\{1,\dots,k\}\rangle}\) — the split “first \(k\) modes vs the rest.” So a tensor has small TT ranks iff all \(d-1\) contiguous prefix cuts have small rank.

Two immediate consequences:

  • The cheap direction of Part 1: contiguous cuts are bipartitions, so by the Lemma every \(r_k \le \operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal T)\). Deep absorbs shallow for free.
  • The blind spot: TT constrains only \(d-1\) special bipartitions out of \(2^{d-1}-1\). A crossing split — say odd modes vs even modes — severs every bond of the chain at once, and nothing in the TT format keeps its rank small: chaining \(k\) crossed bonds can multiply ranks up to \(r^{k}\).

Part 4 of 8

The star witness: a chain of links

Take \(d\) independent “links,” each a \(2\times 2\) identity matrix \(\ \delta_{i,j} = \sum_{c=1}^{2} \boldsymbol e_c\otimes\boldsymbol e_c\), and lay them side by side into an order-\(2d\) tensor with interleaved modes \((i_1, j_1, i_2, j_2, \dots, i_d, j_d)\):

$$\mathcal L_d\,[i_1,j_1,\dots,i_d,j_d] \;=\; \delta_{i_1 j_1}\,\delta_{i_2 j_2}\cdots\,\delta_{i_d j_d} \;=\;\begin{cases}1 & \text{if } i_k=j_k \text{ for all } k\\[2pt] 0 & \text{otherwise.}\end{cases}$$

Because the links are independent, the rank of any unfolding factorizes link by link: a link whose two legs \(i_k, j_k\) land on the same side contributes rank 1, and a severed link (legs on opposite sides) contributes its matrix rank, 2:

$$\operatorname{rank}\bigl((\mathcal L_d)_{\langle S\rangle}\bigr) \;=\; 2^{\#\text{severed links}}.$$

Now read off both ranks:

  • TT is tiny. In the interleaved order, a contiguous cut severs at most one link, so every TT bond is \(\le 2\) (they alternate \(2,1,2,1,\dots\) — the “1” bonds are the gaps between independent links).
  • CP is huge. The crossing split \(S=\{\text{all } i_k\}\) severs all \(d\) links: the unfolding is the \(2^d\times 2^d\) identity matrix, rank \(2^d\). By the Lemma, \(\operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal L_d)\ge 2^d\). And \(2^d\) rank-1 terms suffice (expand each \(\delta\) as \(\sum_c \boldsymbol e_c\otimes\boldsymbol e_c\) and distribute), so $$\operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal L_d) = 2^{d} \qquad\text{while every TT rank} \le 2 .$$

The distributive law, weaponized. \(\mathcal L_d\) is just the product \(\prod_k \delta_{i_k j_k}\) — a “deep” expression with \(d\) tiny factors. Writing it in CP form means expanding the product of sums into a sum of products: \(\prod_{k=1}^{d}\bigl(\sum_{c} e_c\otimes e_c\bigr)\) explodes into \(2^d\) monomials, and the identity-matrix unfolding certifies that no algebraic miracle can collapse them. Depth = keeping things factored; shallowness = being forced to expand.

Part 5 of 8

The numerical courtroom

Claims about rank should survive an optimizer trying its best to refute them. The Lemma even predicts the exact best-possible error of a rank-\(R\) fit: any CP-rank-\(R\) tensor has an odds-vs-evens unfolding of rank \(\le R\), and the best rank-\(R\) approximation of the identity \(\boldsymbol I_N\) (all \(N=2^d\) singular values equal to 1) has Frobenius error \(\sqrt{N-R}\). Truncating the CP expansion of \(\mathcal L_d\) achieves exactly that, so

$$\min_{\operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal S)\le R} \frac{\lVert \mathcal L_d-\mathcal S\rVert_F}{\lVert \mathcal L_d\rVert_F} \;=\;\sqrt{\frac{2^d-R}{2^d}}\;.$$

No loopholes: the bound is a matrix-rank bound, so it also holds for limits of rank-\(R\) tensors (no “border rank” escape). Part 6 of 8

From one example to almost all tensors

The chain of links is not a freak case — it is the typical case. For a tensor built from TT cores with bonds \(\le r\), the odd-vs-even unfolding chains together \(\lfloor d/2\rfloor\) independent bond crossings, and for generic (random) cores the resulting rank reaches \(r^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}\) whenever the mode size is at least \(r\) — exactly what you watched happen in Part 3’s widget (\(2^3 = 8\), every time you reseed). Applying the Lemma:

Theorem (Khrulkov, Novikov & Oseledets, ICLR 2018 — ref. [26] of the review). For almost every order-\(d\) tensor with mode sizes \(n \ge r\) and TT ranks bounded by \(r\) (all except a set of measure zero in the TT parameters): $$\operatorname{rank}_{\mathrm{CP}}(\mathcal T)\;\ge\; r^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}.$$ (KNO’s general bound is \(\min(n,r)^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}\), stated for even \(d\); odd \(d\) follows by fixing one index.) The review paper states this bound as \(r^{d/2}\) in Sec. II-D. The same story holds for hierarchical (HT) formats over trees — that version is the depth-separation theorem of Cohen, Sharir & Shashua (COLT 2016, ref. [10]): generic functions computed by a deep convolutional sum-product network of polynomial size require shallow networks of exponential size.

“Generic/almost every” is doing honest work here: some TT tensors do have small CP rank (a rank-1 tensor is a TT tensor with all bonds 1!). The theorem says such tensors are a measure-zero sliver inside the TT variety — pick TT cores at random and you land outside the sliver with probability one.

The gap, quantified

For mode size \(n=r\): parameters of the deep (TT) representation, \(\approx d\,r^3\), versus the guaranteed minimum number of CP components of a generic such tensor, \(r^{\lfloor d/2\rfloor}\) — each CP component itself costing \(d\,r\) parameters. Log scale: straight line = exponential.

Fine print (what the theorem does not say). (1) It is about exact representation; approximation versions exist but are subtler — Part 5 showed one exact-error computation. (2) TT rank depends on the mode ordering (reorder \(\mathcal L_d\)’s modes so all \(i\)’s come first and its middle TT rank blows up to \(2^{d}\) — that cut now severs all \(d\) links); CP rank is ordering-invariant, so the separation statement itself is not an ordering trick. (3) It concerns sum-product / polynomial architectures. For ReLU networks the picture differs: the review (Sec. V-B, ref. [40]) cautions that a positive-measure set of deep ReLU CNNs can be realized by small shallow ones — expressivity results must be interpreted with care.

Part 7 of 8

What this buys you about neural networks

Sum-product networks (paper eq. 7) compute an inner product between a rank-1 feature tensor and a coefficient tensor \(\mathcal A\):

$$f(\boldsymbol x)\;=\;\bigl\langle\,\mathcal A,\; \phi(x_1)\otimes\phi(x_2)\otimes\cdots\otimes\phi(x_{d_{\mathrm{in}}})\,\bigr\rangle .$$

The architecture is the tensor format of \(\mathcal A\):

  • a shallow network (one hidden layer of product units) parametrizes \(\mathcal A\) in CP form — its width equals the number of CP components;
  • a deep network (hierarchically composing pairs) parametrizes \(\mathcal A\) in HT/TT form — its size tracks the tree’s bond dimensions.

Now the translation is mechanical. Take the deep network computing the chain-of-links function — the product of pairwise feature matchings \(f(\boldsymbol x)=\prod_{k=1}^{d}\bigl\langle\phi(x_{2k-1}),\phi(x_{2k})\bigr\rangle\) (so \(d_{\mathrm{in}} = 2d\) inputs, and \(\mathcal A\) is the order-\(2d\) chain \(\mathcal L_d\)). Deep cost: \(d\) tiny pairwise operations. A shallow network computing the same function must realize \(\mathcal L_d\) as a CP sum — Part 4 proved that takes \(2^d\) product units. And by Part 6 this is not a hand-picked pathology: almost every function a small deep sum-product network computes requires an exponentially wide shallow network. The same rank comparison, run through RNNs with multilinear layers (whose weight tensor is a TT), gives the exponential width separation between generic recurrent and shallow models (ref. [26]); for second-order RNNs, whose coefficient tensor is in CP format, expressivity strictly grows with the rank up to a saturation point (ref. [11]).

The moral. “Depth helps” here is not an intuition — it is a rank inequality. Deep architectures keep the coefficient tensor factored (TT/HT: products along a tree); shallow ones must expand it (CP: one big sum). The distributive law is exponentially expensive to undo, and unfolding ranks are the certificate.
