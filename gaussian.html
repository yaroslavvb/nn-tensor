Compressing Pure Noise: Low-Rank Approximation of a Gaussian Matrix

Take an \(n\times n\) matrix of i.i.d. \(\mathcal N(0,1)\) entries and ask the most basic question in low-rank approximation: how small is the best rank-\(r\) error, and how fast does it decay with \(r\)? The answer is completely explicit — Eckart–Young reduces it to the singular values, the quarter-circle law pins those down, and one trig substitution gives a closed-form error curve with clean power-law asymptotics. Every spectrum on this page is computed live in your browser and laid over the formula.

Part 1 of 5

Eckart–Young + the quarter-circle law: everything is in the spectrum

Write the SVD \(A = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \sigma_j u_j v_j^{\mathsf T}\) with \(\sigma_1 \ge \dots \ge \sigma_n \ge 0\), and let \(A_r = \sum_{j\le r} \sigma_j u_j v_j^{\mathsf T}\) be the rank-\(r\) truncation. The Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem says truncation is optimal in both norms, with error read directly off the discarded singular values:

$$\min_{\operatorname{rank} B \le r}\lVert A - B\rVert_2 = \sigma_{r+1}, \qquad \min_{\operatorname{rank} B \le r}\lVert A - B\rVert_F = \Big(\textstyle\sum_{j>r}\sigma_j^2\Big)^{1/2}.$$

So for a random matrix the whole question is: what does its singular spectrum look like? For i.i.d. \(\mathcal N(0,1)\) entries the answer is a classical limit law: the normalized values \(\sigma_j/\sqrt n\) fill the interval \([0,2]\) with the quarter-circle density

$$\rho(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\sqrt{4 - x^2}, \qquad 0 \le x \le 2,$$

(the Marchenko–Pastur law for \(A^{\mathsf T}A/n\), pushed through \(x = \sqrt\lambda\)). Three consequences to keep in mind: \(\sigma_1 \approx 2\sqrt n\), \(\;\lVert A\rVert_F \approx n\) (exactly, \(\mathbb E\lVert A\rVert_F^2 = n^2\)), and — the point of this page — no decay: the sorted spectrum is a smooth, gapless arc, not a staircase falling off a cliff.

First verdict, by eye. Useful low-rank structure means a spectrum with a few large singular values towering over a fast-decaying tail. Gaussian noise has the opposite: a bulk where every scale between \(0\) and \(2\sqrt n\) is occupied with positive density. Whatever rank you keep, the largest discarded singular value is barely smaller than the smallest kept one.

Part 2 of 5

The error curve: simulation meets formula

Define the two relative errors of the best rank-\(r\) approximation,

$$\mathcal E_2(r) = \frac{\sigma_{r+1}}{\sigma_1}, \qquad \mathcal E_F(r) = \frac{\big(\sum_{j>r}\sigma_j^2\big)^{1/2}}{\big(\sum_{j}\sigma_j^2\big)^{1/2}},$$

both equal to \(1\) at \(r=0\) and \(0\) at \(r=n\). Below, the solid curves are the exact Eckart–Young errors of the sampled matrix from Part 1; the dashed curves are the closed-form quarter-circle prediction derived in Part 3. They agree to about a percent already at \(n=100\) — random matrices self-average ferociously.

Part 3 of 5

The formula: one substitution solves the whole curve

In the large-\(n\) limit, keeping rank \(r = \alpha n\) means keeping every singular value above a threshold \(x_*\) chosen so the quarter-circle puts mass \(\alpha\) above it: \(\alpha = \int_{x_*}^{2}\rho(x)\,dx\). The substitution \(x_* = 2\sin\theta\), \(\theta \in [0, \pi/2]\), makes every integral elementary. The discarded fraction \(q = 1 - \alpha\) parametrizes as

$$q \;=\; \frac{2\theta + \sin 2\theta}{\pi}, \qquad x_* = 2\sin\theta,$$

and the two errors come out in closed form. The spectral error is simply the largest discarded value, \(\sigma_{r+1} \approx \sqrt n\, x_*\); the Frobenius error integrates the discarded energy \(x^2\rho(x)\):

$$\boxed{\;\mathcal E_2 = \sin\theta\;} \qquad\qquad \boxed{\;\mathcal E_F = \sqrt{\frac{2\theta - \tfrac12 \sin 4\theta}{\pi}}\;}$$

with absolute versions \(E_2 \approx 2\sqrt{n}\,\sin\theta\) and \(E_F \approx n\,\mathcal E_F\). (Check the endpoints: \(\theta = \pi/2\) is \(q=1\), errors \(=1\); \(\theta = 0\) is full rank, errors \(=0\).) That's the entire theory — a one-parameter curve \(\big(q(\theta),\ \sin\theta,\ \mathcal E_F(\theta)\big)\). Universality makes the same curve appear for any i.i.d. zero-mean unit-variance entries — Rademacher \(\pm1\), uniform, you name it — while heavy tails or correlated entries break it.

Part 4 of 5

Asymptotics: the decay laws

Expand the closed form at its two ends. Near full rank — discard a small fraction \(q = 1 - r/n \to 0\), so \(\theta \approx \pi q/4\):

$$\mathcal E_2 \;\sim\; \frac{\pi}{4}\,q \;\approx\; 0.785\,q, \qquad \mathcal E_F \;\sim\; \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt 3}\,q^{3/2} \;\approx\; 0.907\,q^{3/2}.$$

Low rank — keep a small fraction \(\alpha = r/n \to 0\), so \(\theta \to \pi/2\):

$$\mathcal E_2 \;\approx\; 1 - \tfrac12\Big(\tfrac{3\pi\alpha}{4}\Big)^{2/3} \;\approx\; 1 - 0.885\,\alpha^{2/3}, \qquad \mathcal E_F \;\approx\; 1 - 2\alpha .$$

The second expansion contains the most quotable fact on this page: squaring it, the energy captured by the best rank-\(r\) approximation is \(\approx 4r/n\). Projecting onto \(r\) random directions captures \(r/n\) in expectation — so the optimal SVD basis beats blind guessing by a factor of four, and no more. And for fixed \(r\) with \(n \to \infty\), both relative errors \(\to 1\): a fixed-rank approximation of large noise is asymptotically worthless.

Power laws on log–log axes

Both regimes, sample (solid) vs formula (dashed) vs the pure power law (gray). On log–log axes a power law is a straight line whose slope is the exponent — watch the sample lock onto slopes \(1\) and \(3/2\) (tail regime), or \(2/3\) and \(1\) (head regime).

What the decay is not. For structured matrices one hopes for \(\sigma_j \lesssim c^{-j}\) (exponential; smooth kernels, analytic functions) or \(\sigma_j \lesssim j^{-s}\) (algebraic; Sobolev-type smoothness), giving errors that collapse at tiny ranks. Gaussian noise offers neither: the error is \(\Theta(1)\) until \(r\) is a constant fraction of \(n\), and even its fastest regime — the final approach to full rank — is only a \(q^{3/2}\) power law in the fraction you threw away. Rank buys you nothing because noise has no preferred directions.

Part 5 of 5

The verdict: noise is incompressible — and that's useful

Three ways to read the result. As a negative: low-rank approximation is a theory of structure, and pure randomness is its exact opposite — the worst case and the random case nearly coincide (this is the matrix shadow of the L² worst-case incompressibility theorem, where the same flat-spectrum logic makes random tensors exponentially incompressible in every format). As a baseline: whenever you truncate real data, the quarter-circle curve is the null hypothesis — any singular value inside the bulk edge \(2\sqrt n\,\hat\sigma_{\text{noise}}\) is indistinguishable from noise, which is exactly the logic behind optimal hard thresholds of the Gavish–Donoho \(4/\sqrt3\) type. As a positive: in the additive model \(\text{data} = \text{signal} + \text{noise}\), the incompressibility of noise is the good news — the SVD can't accidentally "compress" the noise into your kept components; what truncation keeps is (mostly) whatever structure was really there. (And if you measure error by output direction rather than by a norm, the story is harsher still — see the angle deep dive: the worst-case angle is 90° at every rank, while the typical angle is exactly \(\arcsin\mathcal E_F\).)

TL;DR. For an \(n\times n\) Gaussian matrix, the best rank-\(r\) approximation errors are, with \(q = 1 - r/n\) and \(q = (2\theta + \sin2\theta)/\pi\): relative spectral error \(\sin\theta\), relative Frobenius error \(\sqrt{(2\theta - \tfrac12\sin4\theta)/\pi}\). Decay: \(\approx 0.785\,q\) and \(\approx 0.907\,q^{3/2}\) near full rank; at low rank the captured energy is only \(4r/n\) — four times better than random directions — and fixed rank captures nothing as \(n\) grows. No exponential decay exists at any rank: noise has no preferred directions, so rank — the currency of every decomposition on this site — cannot buy any of it.
