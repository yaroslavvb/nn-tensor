01 · The problem

Decades of effort, ~48,000 stable crystals

  • Experiments over decades catalogued ~20,000 computationally stable structures in the ICSD — out of ~200,000 total entries. Costs, throughput and synthesis complications make this route impractical to scale.
  • First-principles pipelines — Materials Project, OQMD, AFLOWLIB, NOMAD — plus simple ion substitutions improved this to ~48,000 computationally stable materials (authors’ recalculation).
  • Chemical-intuition-guided substitution “fundamentally limited how diverse candidates could be”.
  • Machine learning had been ineffective at estimating stability — the decomposition energy against the convex hull of competing phases.

02 · What “stable” means

Stability = sitting on the convex hull

  • For each chemical system, plot formation energy vs composition. The lower convex hull of all competing phases is the stability frontier.
  • The decomposition energy \( \Delta E_{\mathrm{d}} = E - E_{\mathrm{hull}} \) is the distance to that hull: on/below → stable; above → decomposes into a mixture of hull phases.
  • GNoME filters with a 50 meV/atom threshold on predicted decomposition energy — loose on purpose, to improve recall of stable crystals before DFT.
  • Drag the candidate: push it below the hull and the hull itself updates — that is what each of the 381,000 discoveries did.

04 · The models

GNoME: message-passing GNNs that predict energy

  • Structural model: atoms = nodes, edges when closer than 4.0 Å; 256-dim embeddings, shallow swish MLPs, 3 message-passing steps; messages normalized by the dataset’s average adjacency.
  • Compositional model: Roost-style — the reduced formula as a fully connected graph, no structure input.
  • Ensembles of n = 10 networks; the median is the prediction, the interquartile range bounds uncertainty.
  • Test sets split by a composition hash (85/15) — no composition overlap between train and test.

Hover bars. Structural: prior benchmark 28 → 21 on the ~69,000-material 2018 Materials Project snapshot → 11 meV/atom after active learning. Compositional: 60 on noisy MP labels → 40 meV/atom when labelled by ≥10 converged AIRSS runs.

Source: §“GNoME”; Methods “Graph networks”, “Training structural and composition models”, “Composition-based hashing”, Nature s41586-023-06735-9.

06 · Scaling laws

Error falls as a power law — and generalization emerges

  • Test loss “improves as a power law” with data — matching neural scaling laws from language and vision. Unlike language, materials can generate their own new data and keep going.
  • Emergent out-of-distribution generalization: models predict structures with 5+ unique elements accurately, despite those being omitted from training (it stopped at four).
  • On out-of-domain random-structure-search (AIRSS) inputs — high-energy, off-manifold structures — accuracy still climbs steadily with scale: “a substantial step towards a universal energy predictor”.
Source: Fig. 1d,e (point values ≈, read off log–log axes); §“Scaling laws and generalization”, Nature s41586-023-06735-9.
06 · Do the discoveries hold up?

Three independent checks

  • The lab agrees. Candidates derive from March 2021 snapshots; by January 2023 experimentalists had independently made 736 of GNoME’s structures (184 of them after the project began). Of 3,182 compositions newly added to the Materials Project, 2,202 were already in GNoME — with 91% structure agreement.
  • A better functional agrees. Re-checked with r²SCAN: 84% of discovered binaries and ternaries keep negative phase-separation energies (Materials Project manages 90% — at far smaller scale), and 86.8% of tested quaternaries stay on the r²SCAN hull.
  • The distribution agrees. Discovered quaternaries show phase-separation energies distributed like Materials Project’s — “meaningfully stable with respect to competing phases and not just ‘filling’ the convex hull”.

06 · Downstream payoff I

A by-product: state-of-the-art interatomic potentials

  • The hundreds of millions of relaxation steps become pretraining data for a NequIP potential (16.24M parameters, JAX): learned forces for molecular dynamics.
  • Zero-shot — with no training on any AIMD data — it classifies superionic conductors, handles unseen compositions, melts and vacancies, and outperforms general-purpose potentials.
  • Fast: ~14 ms per step for a 50-atom system on one A100 → ~12 ns/day of 2-fs MD.
Source: Fig. 3a–d (point values ≈, read off log axes); §“Scaling up learned interatomic potentials”, Methods “Pretrained GNoME potential”, Nature s41586-023-06735-9.

Limitations

What the paper itself flags

  • Stability is not synthesizability. Open problems: “phase transitions through competing polymorphs, dynamic stability arising from vibrational profiles and configurational entropies and, ultimately, synthesizability.”
  • The hull is a moving target. GNoME structures “could be bumped off the convex hull by future discoveries” — GNoME itself displaced at least 5,000 ‘stable’ materials from Materials Project and OQMD.
  • Functional choice matters. PBE traded accuracy for speed; 16% of binaries/ternaries lose stability under r²SCAN, and PBE–r²SCAN discrepancies remain to be analysed.
  • Simplifications: ferromagnetic spin initialization only (other orderings were computationally prohibitive); for some compositions most AIRSS initializations fail to converge — “why” is unresolved future work.
  • Frozen snapshots: candidates built off March/June 2021 databases; concurrent efforts grew external counts only 35,000 → 48,000 by 2023 (small vs 381,000).
Source: Conclusion; §“Discovered stable crystals”; Methods “Snapshots”, “VASP calculations”, “AIRSS structure generation”; Supplementary Notes 1–2 (as referenced), Nature s41586-023-06735-9.
Takeaways

Three things to remember

  • Discovery is a flywheel, not a filter. Generate diversely (SAPS + compositions), filter with GNN ensembles, verify with DFT, retrain — six rounds took hit rates from <6% to >80% and MAE from 21 to 11 meV/atom, yielding 2.2M stable structures and 381,000 hull entries — an order-of-magnitude expansion.
  • Scaling laws work in materials too — with a twist. Error falls as a power law with data, and unlike language, the pipeline can manufacture its own new data; generalization to 5+-element chemistries emerges without being trained.
  • The data is the second product. Relaxation trajectories pretrain interatomic potentials that run zero-shot MD, screen ionic conductors at AIMD fidelity, and point “towards a universal energy predictor”.
